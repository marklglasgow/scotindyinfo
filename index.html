Scottish Independence

Economic Impact Assessment — Second Edition  ·  April 2026

Net Forecast
Net Negative (Near-term)
⚠ Key Risks
Structural fiscal deficit of £8–17bn from day one. ~60% of exports exposed to rUK border friction. Financial services relocation risk is high and likely partly irreversible.
≈ Base Case
GDP −3% to −8% in years 1–5. Gradual recovery from year 5. Broadly neutral at year 10 if EU access is secured. High uncertainty throughout.
✦ Opportunities
EU single market (450m consumers), largest per-capita renewable energy resource in Europe, full policy autonomy, £0.8–1.8bn/yr in potential defence savings.

Scotland faces a genuinely complex economic calculus. The analysis — spanning 15 sections and drawing on ONS, GERS, IFS, OBR, and IMF data — finds that the economic case for independence is neither straightforwardly strong nor straightforwardly weak.

The near-term picture is clearly negative. The structural fiscal deficit (~9–10% of GDP on a GERS basis), the dominance of rUK trade (~60% of exports), financial sector relocation risk, currency uncertainty, and one-off transition costs of £4.5–9.7bn all materialise simultaneously — before any offsetting benefits from EU membership or policy autonomy can compound.

The long-term picture is uncertain but plausible. Comparable independent economies — Ireland, Denmark, Norway, Finland — demonstrate that viability at Scotland's scale is achievable. Scotland's renewable energy resource base is a genuine structural advantage. The steel-man analysis finds the pro-independence economic case is coherent over a 20-year horizon under favourable conditions.

Analytical verdict: A high-variance proposition. Near-term costs are certain and front-loaded; long-term gains are real but contingent on policy choices, negotiation outcomes, and governance quality that cannot be determined in advance. The outcome depends more on how independence is implemented than on independence itself.

You are an independent economic research analyst tasked with producing a balanced, evidence-based assessment of the potential economic impacts of Scottish independence.

Your analysis must be neutral, rigorous, and grounded in credible data sources (e.g., UK ONS, Scottish Government publications, IFS, Fraser of Allander Institute, academic research, OECD, IMF where relevant).

STRUCTURE YOUR RESPONSE AS FOLLOWS:

1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (max 300 words)
   - Provide a concise, balanced overview of the key economic opportunities and risks
   - Clearly state areas of high uncertainty vs high confidence

2. BASELINE CONTEXT
   - Current structure of Scotland's economy (GDP, key sectors, trade flows)
   - Fiscal position (revenues vs spending, including GERS-style analysis)
   - Relationship with the rest of the UK (rUK) economically

3. KEY ECONOMIC DIMENSIONS OF INDEPENDENCE
   Analyse each of the following areas in a balanced way, outlining both potential
   benefits and risks: Trade & Borders, Currency & Monetary Policy, Fiscal Position,
   Investment & Business Environment, Energy & Natural Resources, Labour Market
   & Migration, Financial Services, Public Services.

4. SECTOR-BY-SECTOR IMPACT ANALYSIS
   For each major sector, assess likely positive and negative impacts across three
   time horizons: Short-term (0–3 yrs), Medium-term (3–10 yrs), Long-term (10+ yrs).

5. SCENARIO ANALYSIS
   Develop at least three plausible scenarios: Optimistic, Central/Base Case,
   Pessimistic. For each include GDP impact, employment effects, public finance
   stability.

6. TRANSITION COSTS & PRACTICAL IMPLEMENTATION

7. COMPARABLE COUNTRY CASE STUDIES (Ireland, Denmark, Norway, Finland)

8. KEY UNCERTAINTIES & ASSUMPTIONS

9. BALANCED CONCLUSION

10. FINAL SUMMARY TABLE
    Area | Likely Positive Impact | Likely Negative Impact | Confidence Level

STYLE REQUIREMENTS:
- Be neutral and analytical, not political
- Clearly distinguish evidence from speculation
- Use ranges and scenarios rather than precise forecasts
- Avoid emotive language
- Where possible, cite or reference known studies or institutions

The second edition was subsequently expanded following peer review feedback, adding sections on Defence & Security Economics, Fiscal Repair Modelling, EU Accession Assessment, Household-Level Impacts, Renewable Revenue Realism, and a Steel-Man analysis of pro-independence economic arguments.
